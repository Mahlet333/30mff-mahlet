About the Film

I thought I came to study (but the campus studied me) is about a student who slowly begins to realize something unsettling. University is not just about classes or deadlines. It is about the spaces you move through every day. At first, those spaces seem ordinary: a dorm room, a humming laundry machine, a flickering hallway light, the soft glow of a laptop screen at three in the morning. But over time, the student senses something else. The campus is not just a backdrop. It is paying attention. It watches. It regulates. It does not ask anything directly, but somehow, you begin to change. Your thoughts. Your rhythms. Even your silences.

The film drifts quietly through these places. There are no fast cuts. No loud sounds. It invites you to slow down and feel. To notice. To ask: what is really shaping me here? This is not a scary story. There are no monsters. No villains. Just architecture. Routine. Quiet pressure.

Shot entirely on location at NYU Abu Dhabi, using real campus spaces, the film tries to capture what it actually feels like to live on campus. Being busy but still stuck. Being watched, but not by cameras. Just by the space itself. The building. The air. The system. The film is short. Only ninety seconds. But it might stay with you longer.

Analysis

Slide 1 — The Golden Grass

The Surface: Sunlight filters through tall grass near a tree. Still, quiet.

What the Campus Was Doing: This isn't just landscaping—it's a mood regulator. The soft movement of grass under light slows your pace without asking.

Missed Detail: The grass moves only on one side of the frame—just enough to feel intentional.

Line from Script: “Today I actually saw it. The light hit different. So did the silence.”

Reflect: How often do you see the space around you instead of just moving through it?

Slide 2 — The Library at 3AM

The Surface: Pale morning light hitting the library's empty courtyard. Stark shadows.

What the Campus Was Doing: Even in silence, the campus reminds you it's awake. Every surface reflects the fact that your body is still here, still working.

Missed Detail: The same student figure appears in three windows in different angles.

Line from Script: “Just finish and move on.”

Reflect: What does it mean to stay longer than your mind wants to?

Slide 3 — The Back Staircase

The Surface: A bare stairwell, lit by a single overexposed light.

What the Campus Was Doing: Movement here is mechanical. It's meant to move you fast—no time for thoughts. Just up or down.

Missed Detail: The stair edges flicker for one second like a skipped frame.

Line from Script: “Do it again. That's the rule, right?”

Reflect: What parts of your life feel like going up stairs that never lead anywhere?

Slide 4 — The Laundry Room

The Surface: A sterile corner with stacked chairs and no people.

What the Campus Was Doing: Designed for solitude, but not comfort. The cold materials, the echoing hum—it's a room that keeps you moving.

Missed Detail: One chair leg hovers slightly off the ground—always off balance.

Line from Script: “Even the laundry has a system. Maybe I needed that too.”

Reflect: When was the last time silence made you uncomfortable?

Slide 5 — The Cat with the Bird

The Surface: A white cat with a black head carries a bird gently in its mouth.

What the Campus Was Doing: This is instinct—not decorum. The campus includes life that doesn't follow the rules of efficiency or order.

Missed Detail: The bird's wing moves once before going still.

Line from Script: “Some things thrive just by being allowed to.”

Reflect: What part of you would survive without needing to be managed?

Slide 6 — The Elevator Wall

The Surface: Scribbled markings scratched into a faded elevator panel.

What the Campus Was Doing: It moves everyone, but remembers no one—unless someone writes it in.

Missed Detail: The word "stay" is etched into the far corner.

Line from Script: “I walked through this a hundred times. Today I actually saw it.”

Reflect: When was the last time you left behind a trace?

Slide 7 — The Griddle Station

The Surface: A gloved hand flips vegetables on a hot grill. Oil crackles.

What the Campus Was Doing: Precision over presence. Everything looks like care, but it's performed to keep things running—not to connect.

Missed Detail: One glove is slightly torn at the wrist.

Line from Script: “This place isn't soft, but it's honest.”

Reflect: When does service feel like routine instead of care?

Slide 8 — The Surveillance Glass

The Surface: A quiet corner of campus just before dusk, camera visible, glass reflecting trees.

What the Campus Was Doing: The camera watches. The glass reflects. Either way—you are seen, or you're mirrored.

Missed Detail: The glass shows two copies of the same student walking in opposite directions.

Line from Script: “It responds. To how you see it. To how you move through it.”

Reflect: Who do you become when the space is silently watching?

Slide 9 — The Dorm Bed

The Surface: A dark bedroom with wrinkled sheets and morning light just peeking through.

What the Campus Was Doing: It gives you the illusion of pause—without comfort. The day already started before you did.

Missed Detail: The water bottle by the window is untouched in every scene.

Line from Script: “Sleep when I can. Wake up late.”

Reflect: What does your bed know about you that your friends don't?

Slide 10 — The Study Glow

The Surface: A glowing laptop screen is the only light in the room. The user sits in near darkness.

What the Campus Was Doing: It teaches you to chase deadlines in the dark, to keep showing up even when you've disappeared from yourself.

Missed Detail: The screen briefly shows the word "break" before switching tabs.

Line from Script: “Back-to-back deadlines. No room to think.”

Reflect: What would you say if the glow went away?

About the Filmmaker

Mahlet Atresaw Andarge is a filmmaker and student at NYU Abu Dhabi. Her work explores the subtle ways environments shape our inner lives, with a focus on memory, routine, and the architecture of feeling. She believes the spaces we inhabit are never neutral—they watch, they remember, and they change us.
